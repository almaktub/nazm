استعاره · Isti'ara

Drop the "Like." Make a Claim.
استعاره (Isti'ara)

A simile says "almost." A metaphor says "yes." That one-word difference is the distance between a description and a declaration, and it changes everything about how a line lands.

Start with a simile: "Her hair is like a river of darkness." Fine. Now remove the "like", "Her hair is a river of darkness." That's the first step toward Isti'ara (استعاره), what Persian rhetoric calls metaphor. Now go further: don't mention hair at all. "This dark river flows past her shoulders." You've stopped naming the subject. The metaphor has completely taken over.

That full takeover is what Isti'ara does at its most powerful. The vehicle (the comparison) replaces the subject so completely that the literal thing disappears. When Rumi writes about a reed flute crying, he's not mentioning the soul. When Hafez writes about wine, he's often not talking about wine. The comparison has absorbed the subject.

Isti'ara-ye Tasrihiyya, The Open Metaphor

The comparison is stated outright; the subject is hidden. "The lion entered the room", meaning a brave person. "Lion" is present; the brave person is implied. The most common English metaphor works exactly this way.

Isti'ara-ye Makniyya, The Implied Metaphor

The subject is named but the vehicle is hidden, only hinted at through one of its properties. "Death stretched out its hands", death is the subject, but the hidden vehicle (a predator, a reaper) appears only through the gesture. More haunting, more atmospheric.

The Reed Flute, The Most Famous Isti'ara in Persian

بشنو این نی چون شکایت می‌کند
از جدایی‌ها حکایت می‌کند
کز نیستان تا مرا ببریده‌اند
از نفیرم مرد و زن نالیده‌اند
سینه خواهم شرحه شرحه از فراق
تا بگویم شرح درد اشتیاق
"Listen to the reed flute, how it tells a tale of separations / Since they cut me from the reed bed / Every man and woman has mourned alongside me / I want a chest torn open by separation / So I can explain the pain of longing.", Rumi, opening of the Masnavi. The reed flute is the human soul. The reed bed is God, the origin, the place of union. Being cut from it is the fall into embodied existence. By the time Rumi has finished his first eighteen couplets, you've forgotten it's even a comparison.

"آتش عشق است کاندر نی فتاد
جوشش عشق است کاندر می فتاد
نی حریف هر که از یاری برید
پرده‌هایش پرده‌های ما درید"

Rumi, Masnavi, "It is the fire of Love that fell into the reed / It is the ferment of Love that fell into the wine. / The reed is the companion of anyone cut from a friend / Its melodies tore open our veils." Two isti'aras in one couplet: the reed as soul, the wine as love. The hidden vehicles (fire, ferment) suggest something alchemical and impossible to contain. Notice how Rumi uses both the reed and wine, two of the most charged symbols in all of Persian poetry, in the same breath.

John Donne and the Extended Isti'ara

The English poets closest to the Persian metaphor tradition are the Metaphysicals, Donne, Herbert, Marvell. They shared the Persian taste for the conceit: a metaphor stretched so far, through such unexpected connections, that it risks breaking, but somehow doesn't.

🇮🇷 Extended Isti'ara, Hafez
"می‌خور که عقل و دین را خواهی نشان دادن
این هر دو را شراب است آیینه‌ی تمام
ای دل غم این کار مخور یک جرعه می بزن
کاین عقده با ورع نگشاید ولی با می"
"Drink wine, if you want to reveal reason and faith / For wine is the perfect mirror of both. / O heart, don't grieve this, take one sip of wine / This knot won't be untied by piety, but by wine." Hafez extends the wine isti'ara until it becomes a mirror for the entire spiritual life. The metaphor absorbs both rationality and religion. The vehicle (wine) has replaced the tenor (spiritual experience) so completely that they're indistinguishable.
🎭 Extended Metaphor, John Donne
"No man is an island entire of itself;
every man is a piece of the continent,
a part of the main;
if a clod be washed away by the sea, Europe is the less"
Donne, "Meditation XVII" (1624). A full extended metaphor: humanity as geography. The continent, the main, the clod washed away by the sea, each element adds a new layer to the same central image. Structurally, this is identical to Rumi's reed: one vehicle (geography) absorbs a complex subject (human connection and mortality) completely.

Hip-Hop Metaphor: Compressed and Immediate

If classical Persian isti'ara tends toward the extended and the spiritual, hip-hop tends toward the compressed and the material. But the move is the same, the vehicle replaces the tenor, the comparison word is gone, and the listener does the work.

🇦🇫 Isti'ara-ye Makniyya, Bedil (Dari)
"زمانه خوابگه ما و مرگ بیداری است
بگو چه وقت کند خواب این نفس که بیداری است
از خود شو تا بدانی آنچه گفتم
که عالم خواب ماست و ما بیداریم"
"The world is our sleeping place, and death is the awakening / Tell me, when will this conscious breath finally sleep? / Lose yourself before you understand what I've said / The world is our dream and we are awake in it.", Bedil (1644–1720). Life as sleep, death as waking. The vehicle (a dream) is hidden, implied only through the behavior described. The reversal of the usual fear-of-death through a single implied metaphor.
🎤 Compressed Isti'ara, The Notorious B.I.G.
"It was all a dream
I used to read Word Up magazine
Salt-N-Pepa and Heavy D up in the limousine
Hangin' pictures on my wall"
Biggie, "Juicy" (1994). "It was all a dream", three words, a complete isti'ara of the entire narrative. Life as dream. Success as dream. The vehicle has replaced the tenor. Bedil and Biggie are working in the same vein: separated by three centuries and ten thousand miles, both reached for the same image to describe the distance between what was and what is.
🇮🇷 Googoosh, Iranian pop (1970s–now)
"تو رفتی و من موندم با این خونه‌ی سرد
که دیوارش آروم می‌گیره درد
دلم پیشته اما تنم اینجاست
دو نیمه شدم وقتی رفتی از کنارم"
"You left and I stayed with this cold house / Whose walls quietly absorb the pain / My heart is with you but my body is here / I was split in two when you left my side.", Googoosh, Iran's most iconic singer. The cold house as a metaphor for an empty life, an isti'ara-ye makniyya where the vehicle (the house) carries the weight of loss. A decade of grief compressed into an image of a room.
🎵 Adele, Someone Like You (2011)
"I hate to turn up out of the blue uninvited
But I couldn't stay away, I couldn't fight it
I had hoped you'd see my face and that you'd be reminded
That for me it isn't over"
The whole song is an implied metaphor: the singer as someone who hasn't moved on, the meeting as a wound being reopened. Adele never says "I am still broken", she shows up at the door and that gesture is the metaphor. The action replaces the description. Googoosh's house and Adele's door are the same isti'ara, in different cities, different centuries.

Why "Is" Hits Harder Than "Like"

Persian criticism made this argument precisely: isti'ara is stronger than tashbih because it makes a stronger claim. A simile says "these two things are similar." A metaphor says "these two things are each other." One is a comparison, the other is an assertion. The listener has to reckon with an assertion in a way they don't have to with a comparison.

The English philosopher Max Black made almost exactly this argument in 1955: metaphors don't just compare but actively create new meanings by "filtering" one concept through another. He didn't cite Persian rhetoric. He didn't need to. He was describing the same phenomenon, and arriving at the same conclusion a thousand years later.

⚡

The one-line version: Tashbih says "almost." Isti'ara says "yes." The gap between them is exactly where poetry happens.
