Drop the "Like." Make a Claim.
ЎІЎ≥Ў™ЎєЎІЎ±ўЗ (Isti'ara)

A simile says "almost." A metaphor says "yes." That one-word difference is the distance between a description and a declaration, and it changes everything about how a line lands.

Start with a simile: "Her hair is like a river of darkness." Fine. Now remove the "like", "Her hair is a river of darkness." That's the first step toward Isti'ara (ЎІЎ≥Ў™ЎєЎІЎ±ўЗ), what Persian rhetoric calls metaphor. Now go further: don't mention hair at all. "This dark river flows past her shoulders." You've stopped naming the subject. The metaphor has completely taken over.

That full takeover is what Isti'ara does at its most powerful. The vehicle (the comparison) replaces the subject so completely that the literal thing disappears. When Rumi writes about a reed flute crying, he's not mentioning the soul. When Hafez writes about wine, he's often not talking about wine. The comparison has absorbed the subject.

Isti'ara-ye Tasrihiyya, The Open Metaphor

The comparison is stated outright; the subject is hidden. "The lion entered the room", meaning a brave person. "Lion" is present; the brave person is implied. The most common English metaphor works exactly this way.

Isti'ara-ye Makniyya, The Implied Metaphor

The subject is named but the vehicle is hidden, only hinted at through one of its properties. "Death stretched out its hands", death is the subject, but the hidden vehicle (a predator, a reaper) appears only through the gesture. More haunting, more atmospheric.

John Donne and the Extended Isti'ara

The English poets closest to the Persian metaphor tradition are the Metaphysicals, Donne, Herbert, Marvell. They shared the Persian taste for the conceit: a metaphor stretched so far, through such unexpected connections, that it risks breaking, but somehow doesn't.

рЯО≠ Extended Metaphor, John Donne
"No man is an island entire of itself;
every man is a piece of the continent,
a part of the main;
if a clod be washed away by the sea, Europe is the less"
Donne, "Meditation XVII" (1624). A full extended metaphor: humanity as geography. The continent, the main, the clod washed away by the sea, each element adds a new layer to the same central image. Structurally, this is identical to Rumi's reed: one vehicle (geography) absorbs a complex subject (human connection and mortality) completely.

Hip-Hop Metaphor: Compressed and Immediate

If classical Persian isti'ara tends toward the extended and the spiritual, hip-hop tends toward the compressed and the material. But the move is the same, the vehicle replaces the tenor, the comparison word is gone, and the listener does the work.

"Life in the world is but a great dream;

"Spring Day: Waking from a Dream," Li Bai, Tang Dynasty. Life as dream, one complete isti'ara, and then the poem proves the metaphor by acting it out: he doesn't work, doesn't worry, lies at the door and drinks. Bedil's cosmological reversal and Li Bai's domestic one reach for the same image from different directions: the waking state may be the real sleep, and the world is not what it insists it is.

рЯУЦ Implied Metaphor, Emily Dickinson
"After great pain, a formal feeling comesвАФ
The Nerves sit ceremonious, like TombsвАФ
The stiff Heart questions, was it He, that bore,
And Yesterday, or Centuries before?"
Dickinson, c. 1862. The whole poem is an implied metaphor: grief as a kind of numbness that performs normality. The Nerves become ceremonious (like mourners at a funeral). The Heart becomes a questioner that has lost track of time. No one says "I am grieving." The poem's own stiffness, its formal grammar, is the metaphor, the vehicle is the poem itself. Googoosh's cold house and Dickinson's formal ceremony are the same isti'ara-ye makniyya, the empty room and the stiff procedure both saying the thing that can't be said directly.

Isti'ara-ye Tamsiliyya, The Allegory

Stretch an isti'ara far enough and it stops being a single metaphor and becomes a whole dramatized scene that means something else, start to finish. Persian calls this isti'ara-ye tamsiliyya, the allegorical metaphor. A tavern, a painter's studio, a reed bed: the surface is one thing, every prop in it points to another.

Why "Is" Hits Harder Than "Like"

Persian criticism made this argument precisely: isti'ara is stronger than tashbih because it makes a stronger claim. A simile says "these two things are similar." A metaphor says "these two things are each other." One is a comparison, the other is an assertion. The listener has to reckon with an assertion in a way they don't have to with a comparison.

The English philosopher Max Black made almost exactly this argument in 1955: metaphors don't just compare but actively create new meanings by "filtering" one concept through another. He didn't cite Persian rhetoric. He didn't need to. He was describing the same phenomenon, and arriving at the same conclusion a thousand years later.
