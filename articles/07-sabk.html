سبک · Sab'k

Every Era Sounds Like Something
سبک (Sabk)

Persian literary history has three great styles across a thousand years. English has its own. Ahmad Zahir vs. Farhad Darya. The Romantics vs. the Metaphysicals. Same argument, every generation.

Every era writes differently, not just in content, but in texture. The density of the imagery. How abstract or concrete the language is. The relationship between sound and sense. The emotional temperature of the whole thing. Persian criticism has a word for this: sab'k (سبک), style.

The Persian tradition maps its history through three dominant styles. English literary history does something similar with different names. Put them next to each other and you see what style actually is, not just aesthetics, but a whole philosophy of what language is for.

Style One: Khorasani, Direct, Clear, Energetic (9th–12th Century)

Khorasan, spanning modern Iran, Afghanistan, and Central Asia, gave Persian poetry its first major flowering. The Khorasani style is direct, concrete, energetic. The imagery is drawn from the physical world: mountains, horses, wine, roses, the hunt. The meters are vigorous. The vocabulary is relatively plain. When a hero is brave, the poet says so with a strong image and moves on, no layers of mystical meaning, no philosophical scaffolding.

🏔️ Khorasani, Rudaki (c. 940 CE)
"بوی جوی مولیان آید همی
یاد یار مهربان آید همی
ریگ آموی و درشتی راه او
زیر پایم پرنیان آید همی"
"The scent of the Moliyan stream keeps coming / The memory of a kind friend keeps coming / The sand of the Amu and the roughness of its road / Feels like silk under my feet.", Rudaki, the oldest surviving major Persian poem. Direct sensory imagery, scent, memory, texture underfoot. No mystical allegory, no conceptual density. The clarity IS the beauty. Feel the longing for home in the sand and the scent.
📖 English parallel, Anglo-Saxon / Early English
"The sea surged, the wave beat shore
The world's candle shone, the wind was still
He saw the headlands gleam, the cliffs grow bright
And steered his ship toward where the light was spilt"
Old English poetic tradition (Beowulf-era). Same Khorasani directness, compound images, physical world as emotional register, no abstraction. "World's candle" for the sun: two concrete nouns, one strong image, nothing hidden. These two traditions, separated by thousands of miles, landed on the same aesthetic instinct independently.

Style Two: 'Araqi, Layered, Spiritual, Doubly-Encoded (12th–15th Century)

This is the era most Western readers know: Rumi, Sa'di, Hafez. The imagery is still there, but now it carries double weight. Wine is wine AND divine ecstasy. The garden is a garden AND paradise AND the beloved's face. The poetry becomes doubly-encoded: love poetry on the surface, spiritual poetry underneath. Both readings are valid. That doubleness IS the style.

🌹 'Araqi, Hafez (14th century)
"دلم جز مهر مه رویان طریقی برنمی‌گیرد
ز هر در می‌زنم چون مهر بر لب قفل می‌گیرد
خدایا آن که رخسارش ببست راه عقل و دین
ببند این زلف دامان‌گیر را بگشا دلم"
"My heart takes no path other than the love of moon-faced ones / From every door I try, the lock falls silent like a seal on lips. / O God, the one whose face blocked the path of reason and religion / Bind up those hem-catching curls and open my heart." Multiple simultaneous layers: literal romantic frustration, spiritual seeking, and a joke about desire blocking reason. All three active at once. That's the 'Araqi style in practice.
🎭 English parallel, Metaphysical Poets (17th century)
"Mark but this flea, and mark in this,
How little that which thou deny'st me is;
It sucked me first, and now sucks thee,
And in this flea our two bloods mingled be"
John Donne. The Metaphysical conceit works exactly like 'Araqi double-coding: a mundane, almost absurd vehicle (a flea) carries enormous erotic and philosophical freight. You track two registers simultaneously, what he's literally saying and what he actually means. The style demands both at once. It's disconcerting and delicious in exactly the same way as Hafez.

Style Three: Hindi, Dense, Strange, Compressed (16th–18th Century)

When the Mughal empire brought Persian poets to India, something interesting happened. Cut off from the Persian heartland, in dialogue with Sanskrit aesthetics, the style changed. The Sab'k-e Hindi is characterized by intellectual density, unusual conceits, and a preference for the surprising over the beautiful. Images get stranger, more compressed, more conceptual.

Bedil (Mirza Abdul Qadir Bedil, 1644–1720), born in what is now Uttar Pradesh, hugely influential in Afghanistan, is the supreme example. His poetry can be almost impenetrable on first read, then flash open with sudden clarity. It's the Persian equivalent of Gerard Manley Hopkins: concentrated, difficult, grammatically unusual. When it works, it's unforgettable.

🇦🇫 Hindi Style, Bedil (Dari/Persian)
"نه ما دانیم و نه ایشان که ما را کی رها کردند
در این وحشت‌سرا دل‌های ما دیوانه‌وار آمد
نه از خود بود و نه از غیر بود این آمدن ما
همان مهمان‌نوازی بود که دعوت داشت"
"Neither we know, nor they, when they released us / In this wilderness our hearts arrived like madmen / Our coming was neither from ourselves nor from another, / It was only a hospitality that had issued an invitation." Dense, questioning, existential. The "they" who released us is ambiguous (God? fate? parents?). The "wilderness" is both the literal world and an interior state. Everything compressed to near-breaking. Sab'k-e Hindi.
📖 English parallel, Gerard Manley Hopkins
"I am soft sift
In an hourglass, at the wall
Fast, but mined with a motion, a drift,
And it crowds and it combs to the fall"
Hopkins, "The Wreck of the Deutschland." Grammatically strange, conceptually dense, the self as sand, the hourglass as life, the falling as inevitability. The difficulty is the point: the resistance of the language mirrors the resistance of the subject matter. Hopkins and Bedil never encountered each other's work. They arrived at the same aesthetic instinct from different corners of a century.

The Style Debate in Afghan Music

Style history isn't just literary, it's alive in how contemporary Afghan music sounds. Two artists make the contrast vivid:

🎵 'Araqi Style, Ahmad Zahir (1946–1979)
"مه رویا، مه رویا، کجا رفتی؟
دل من، دل من، چرا رفتی؟
نه گفتی، نه پرسیدی، نه برگشتی
کجا رفتی که من بی‌تو نتوانم"
"O moon-faced one, where did you go? / My heart, my heart, why did you leave? / You didn't say, didn't ask, didn't return / Where did you go that I cannot be without you?", Ahmad Zahir drew directly on classical 'Araqi imagery (the moon-faced beloved, the suffering heart) but delivered it through warm, Western-influenced vocal arrangements. Classically coded, emotionally direct, universally beloved across generations.
🎵 Contemporary, Farhad Darya (1962–)
"آزادی، آزادی
افغانستان، وطنم
سبزه و کوه و دشت و دریای تو
هر گوشه‌ی تو باغ و گلستان"
"Freedom, freedom / Afghanistan, my homeland / Your greenery, mountains, plains and rivers / Every corner of you is a garden and rose-meadow.", Farhad Darya's post-war work trades classical double-coded imagery for direct address: freedom, homeland, resilience. The figurative scaffolding is lighter; the political urgency is higher. A move from 'Araqi elaborateness toward Khorasani directness, made necessary by the times.

"We are such stuff as dreams are made on,
and our little life
is rounded with a sleep."

Shakespeare, The Tempest, late style. No Badi' decoration, no elaborated simile. Just the compressed metaphor (life as dream, death as sleep surrounding it) delivered with devastating simplicity. Shakespeare's late style and late Bedil's work move in the same direction: the ornament burns away, and only the essential image remains. The most sophisticated thing both traditions do is eventually learn to say less.
