Three Words That Measure Everything
معانی · بیان · بدیع

Ma'ani, Bayan, Badi'. The framework Persian literary theory runs on, and what it actually means when you strip the jargon away.

If you wanted to teach someone to write beautifully, you could say "write beautifully" and hope for the best. Or you could break it down. Persian and Arabic scholars, starting around the 9th century, chose the second option, and went very, very deep.

They split the whole science of eloquent writing into three branches. Together, these are called ʿUlūm al-Balāgha, the Sciences of Rhetoric. Nasrollah Taqavi's Hanjaar-e Goftar (The Art of Speech, 1939) organizes its 350 pages entirely around this three-part framework. Every rule, every example, every device maps to one of them.

Once you see the map, you realize English teachers have been talking about the same three things the whole time, they just never gave them this clean a set of names.

Science One: Ma'ani, What Is Your Sentence Actually Doing?

Ma'ani (علم معانی) means "meanings." It studies how the structure of a sentence, not just the words, shapes what it does to the reader. The same information can be delivered as a command, a question, a suggestion, or a statement, and each of those choices produces a different effect. Ma'ani is the study of those choices.

"Can you pass the salt?" is technically a yes/no question. Everyone knows it means "please pass the salt." Ma'ani is the science of that gap, between what a sentence looks like on the surface and what it's actually doing. It covers things like:

  • Ijaz va Itnab, knowing when to be brief vs. when to develop an idea (knowing when to shut up, basically)
  • Taqdim va Ta'khir, word order, and how moving things around shifts emphasis
  • Maqam, register; speaking appropriately for the occasion and audience
  • Khabar va Insha, the difference between making a statement and issuing a command or question
🇮🇷 Ma'ani in action, Hafez
"الا یا ایها الساقی ادر کأسا و ناولها
که عشق آسان نمود اول ولی افتاد مشکل‌ها
به بوی نافه‌ای کاخر صبا زان طرّه بگشاید
ز تاب جعد مشکینش چه خون افتاد در دل‌ها"
"Come, cupbearer, pour the cup and pass it round / For love seemed easy at first, but then difficulties fell. / In hope that the dawn breeze might finally open that curl / Oh, what blood has fallen in hearts from those dark twisting locks.", An imperative opening sentence. Not a request, a command. The form carries urgency, desire, and the whole weight of what follows. That's Ma'ani: the sentence structure is doing emotional work.
🎭 Ma'ani in action, Shakespeare
"To be, or not to be, that is the question:
Whether 'tis nobler in the mind to suffer
The slings and arrows of outrageous fortune,
Or to take arms against a sea of troubles"
Hamlet, Act III. A question that isn't really a question, it's opening a void. The interrogative structure does philosophical and emotional work that a statement couldn't. Persian Ma'ani scholars would have a field day with this: a question used as an existential declaration.

Science Two: Bayan, How Did You Paint That?

Bayan (علم بیان) means "expression." This is the science of figurative language, how to say one thing by talking about something else. This is the most recognizable branch to anyone who's been through high school English: simile, metaphor, metonymy, allusion. All four live here.

"ز کوی یار می‌آید نسیم باد نوروزی
از این باد ار مدد خواهی چراغ دل فروزی
چه مستی است ندانم که رو به ما آورد
که بوی باده از هر سوی می‌وزد از صبا"

Hafez, "From the street of the Beloved comes the New Year breeze / From this wind, if you seek help, the candle of the heart lights. / What intoxication this is, I don't know, that comes toward us, / The scent of wine blows from every direction on the morning breeze." Every image in this couplet comes from Bayan's toolkit: the breeze as a divine messenger (metaphor), the heart as a candle (metaphor), the New Year wind as spiritual renewal (extended metaphor). Four lines, the whole system in use.

Science Three: Badi', The Part That Makes You Remember It

Badi' (علم بدیع) means "innovative" or "beautiful." If Ma'ani is the architecture and Bayan is the view, Badi' is the detail work, the layer of craft that makes a line stay in your head. It covers two categories: devices that work through sound, and devices that work through meaning.

Badi' Lafzi, Sound Devices

Tajnis (pun, two words that sound the same, mean different things), Saj' (rhymed prose, rhythm and rhyme used in non-poetry writing), Radd al-Ajz (repeating the opening word at a line's end). These are what you feel before you understand them.

Badi' Ma'nawi, Meaning Devices

Tibaq (antithesis, pairing opposites so they charge each other), Mura'at al-Nazir (using words from the same semantic family, putting "sword," "shield," "blood," and "warrior" in one poem because they all belong together), Husn-e Ta'lil (giving a beautiful, poetic reason for something instead of the real one).

🇵🇰 Badi', Mirza Ghalib (Urdu)
"ہزاروں خواہشیں ایسی کہ ہر خواہش پے دم نکلے
بہت نکلے میرے ارمان لیکن پھر بھی کم نکلے
نکالنا بھی تمہیں یاد آ گیا آخرکار
ملے تھے تم تو اس طرح کہ دل لگا بیٹھے"
"Thousands of desires, each worthy of dying for / Many were fulfilled, yet I wish for more / You finally remembered to send me away, / You had met me in such a way that my heart became attached." The antithesis of "many/yet still few" and the wordplay on dying-for-desire are Badi' working at full strength. The final couplet turns the screw: the very attachment is a wound.
🎤 Badi', Jay-Z
"I'm not a businessman
I'm a business, man
Let me handle my business, damn
Freeway, let me know what's happening"
Jay-Z, "Diamonds from Sierra Leone" remix. The comma turns one phrase into two meanings, same sounds, completely different grammatical structure. Persian Badi' scholars called this tajnis-e muraqqab (compound pun). Ghalib would have appreciated the efficiency. Four lines, one cognitive double-exposure, and the point landed before the listener could blink.

Why Three? Why Not Just "Good Writing"?

The three-science framework lets you diagnose what kind of problem a piece of writing has. A sentence can be structurally sound (good Ma'ani) but use clumsy imagery (weak Bayan). A ghazal can have brilliant metaphors (strong Bayan) but feel musically flat (weak Badi'). The framework gives you precision. You're not just saying "something's off", you're saying exactly what's off.

Western rhetoric had the same instinct. Cicero divided good speaking into: finding ideas (inventio), arranging them (dispositio), expressing them beautifully (elocutio), memorizing them (memoria), and delivering them (pronuntiatio). Different categories, same impulse: break down what makes language work so you can study it, teach it, and do it better.

🔑

One-line version: Ma'ani is what your sentence is doing. Bayan is how you painted the idea. Badi' is the extra sparkle that makes it stick. All three are in every great poem. The difference between traditions is how obsessively each one is named, sorted, and debated.
