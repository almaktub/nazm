مجاز و کنایه · Majaz va Kinaya

Hafez Was Probably Not Talking About Wine
مجاز و کنایه (Majaz va Kinaya)

Majaz and Kinaya, the art of saying one thing while meaning another. Plausible deniability, double meaning, and why the most honest writing is sometimes the most indirect.

Sometimes the most powerful sentence is the one that doesn't commit. Not because the writer is vague, but because the indirection itself is doing something the direct statement couldn't. It implicates the reader. It creates a felt knowledge rather than a stated one. It gives both writer and reader cover.

Persian rhetoric has two tools for this. Majaz (مجاز) is using a word in a non-literal sense because of a real, recognizable connection between its literal meaning and what you intend, what English calls metonymy or synecdoche. Kinaya (کنایه) is saying something literally true while meaning something else entirely, implication rather than substitution. Both are everywhere in classical Persian poetry. Both show up in hip-hop and pop music constantly.

Majaz, When One Word Stands in for Its Neighbor

"The White House announced today", the building didn't speak. The government did. The building stands in for the institution because there's a real, recognizable relationship between them. That's majaz. The substitution works because the connection is genuine, not arbitrary, not random.

🌿 Majaz as Ethics, Sa'di
"بنی‌آدم اعضای یک پیکرند
که در آفرینش ز یک گوهرند
چو عضوی به درد آورد روزگار
دگر عضوها را نماند قرار"
"The children of Adam are parts of one body / Created from one essence / When one member suffers with pain / The other members cannot rest.", Sa'di, Golestan. "Members of one body" is synecdoche (part for whole) turned into an ethical argument. The physical relationship carries a moral imperative. This is now on the wall of the United Nations, which is either very fitting or deeply ironic, depending on the day you're reading the news.
🎤 Majaz in Hip-Hop
"The pen is mightier than the sword.
I got the streets on lock.
The crown weighs heavy.
We bleeding the same color."
Pen for the writer's full creative and moral power. Streets for their people and their respect. Crown for the burden of leadership. Bleeding the same for shared humanity. All majaz, one concrete word standing in for a larger reality through a connection that's real, not made up. This language runs through hip-hop because it runs through human expression everywhere and always has.

Kinaya, Saying It Without Saying It

Kinaya is trickier and more interesting than majaz. It's not substitution, it's saying something literally true while meaning something completely different. "He has long hands", literally possible. But in Persian and Arabic idiom, it implies either generosity (his hands reach far to give) or dishonesty (his hands reach into things they shouldn't). Both literal readings work. The implication is what the writer intends.

This gives kinaya something no other device has: plausible deniability baked into the poem itself.

😏

This is exactly why Hafez's enemies couldn't censor him. His poems were technically about wine, taverns, and pretty faces, all forbidden, none of it explicitly political or heretical. But everyone understood what they were really about: spiritual longing, resistance to religious hypocrisy, the gap between outward piety and inner truth. The literal reading was always available as cover. Poetry as legal defense. There's something beautiful and a little reckless about that.

The Hafez Wine Problem

Hafez's Divan, consulted like an oracle for 600 years, sitting next to the Quran in households across the Persian-speaking world, is soaked in wine, taverns, and cupbearers. All technically forbidden in Islam. Scholars have argued for centuries: is this literal? Mystical? Both?

The honest answer: yes to both, simultaneously, on purpose. That simultaneous double reading is kinaya at its most sophisticated.

الا یا ایها الساقی ادر کأسا و ناولها
که عشق آسان نمود اول ولی افتاد مشکل‌ها
ما در پیاله عکس رخ یار دیده‌ایم
ای بی‌خبر ز لذت شرب مدام ما
"Come, O Cupbearer, pass the cup and offer it / For love seemed easy at first, but then difficulties fell upon us. / We have seen the reflection of the Beloved's face in the cup, / O you who are unaware of the joy of our constant drinking." The cupbearer (saqi) is literally a wine-server; implied: a divine intermediary, a Sufi guide. The wine (mai) is literally fermented grape; implied: ecstasy, divine knowledge, the dissolving of the ego. He doesn't ask you to choose a reading. The poem lives in both at once.
🇮🇷 Kinaya, Hafez
"می‌خور که عقل و دین را خواهی نشان دادن
این هر دو را شراب است آیینه‌ی تمام
ای دل غم این کار مخور یک جرعه می بزن
کاین عقده با ورع نگشاید ولی با می"
"Drink wine, if you want to reveal reason and faith / For wine is the perfect mirror of both. / O heart, don't grieve this, take one sip of wine / This knot won't be untied by piety, but by wine." The "knot" is both personal anguish and the theological gap between law and love. The wine is both literal and spiritual. And Hafez names himself committing the forbidden act while defending it philosophically. The pun is the argument.
🎵 Kinaya, Bob Dylan
"The answer, my friend, is blowin' in the wind
The answer is blowin' in the wind
How many years can some people exist
before they're allowed to be free?"
"Blowin' in the Wind." The wind is kinaya: literally air movement, implied something at once everywhere and impossible to grab, justice, peace, the moral obligation of the powerful. The answer IS out there. It just won't stay still long enough to be caught. Dylan's wind and Hafez's wine are the same device: one thing standing in for another, with the literal reading intact for cover.

Ghalib's Wounds, Kinaya in Urdu

📖 Kinaya, Ghalib (Urdu)
"دل ڈھونڈتا ہے پھر وہی فرصتِ شب و روز
بیٹھے رہیں تصوّرِ جانان کیے ہوئے
نادان ہو جو کہتے ہو کیوں زندہ ہیں غالب
قسمت میں ہے مرنا تو ابھی تک نہیں مرے"
"The heart again searches for that leisure of night and day / To sit absorbed in the image of the beloved. / You are foolish to ask why Ghalib is still alive, / If death is my fate, I simply haven't died yet." The final couplet is pure kinaya: literally, Ghalib explains his survival through logic. Actually, he's describing the unbearable persistence of longing, living is the wound, dying would be the relief. The logic covers the grief.
📖 Kinaya, William Blake
"I wander thro' each charter'd street,
Near where the charter'd Thames does flow,
And mark in every face I meet
Marks of weakness, marks of woe."
Blake, "London" (1794). "Charter'd" means both surveyed (put on a map) and legally owned. The literal meaning (streets on a city plan) is true. The implied meaning (a city where everything, including the river, has been turned into property) is what Blake intends. The repetition of "marks" at the end works the same way: facial expressions on the surface, invisible wounds of a society underneath. Blake and Hafez are both writing things that mean two things simultaneously and refusing to resolve the ambiguity. The literal reading is always available as cover. The real reading is everything underneath it.
🇦🇫 Kinaya, Ahmad Zahir
"دلم گرفته از این شهر پر ملال و غبار
کجاست آن رفیق همدرد و آن دیار
دیگر نه آن وطن است و نه آن یار
نه آن چراغ روشن و نه آن بهار"
"My heart is heavy in this sad, dusty city / Where is that understanding friend, that homeland / There is no longer that homeland, nor that friend / Neither that bright light nor that spring." Ahmad Zahir singing in the 1970s, "this city" is literally Kabul. But "that homeland" is kinaya for something more than a place: a state of being, a time before loss, a self before war. The song means both the city and everything the city can no longer hold.
🎵 Kinaya, The Beatles
"Yesterday, all my troubles seemed so far away
Now it looks as though they're here to stay
Oh I believe in yesterday
Suddenly I'm not half the man I used to be"
"Yesterday" is kinaya: literally the previous day, actually the whole prior self, the whole prior life. "Not half the man I used to be" is kinaya working twice, literally a statement about diminishment, actually a statement about grief and self-fracture. McCartney and Ahmad Zahir are writing the same song in different decades, different alphabets, the same feeling.

Kinaya at the Scale of a Song

The most sophisticated kinaya isn't a line or a couplet — it's a whole narrative that carries two complete readings simultaneously for its entire length. The literal story works. The implied story works. And when the implied story finally surfaces, it recontextualizes everything that came before it.

🇮🇷 Extended Kinaya, Hafez (wine = divine love)
"الا یا ایها الساقی ادر کأسا و ناولها
که عشق آسان نمود اول ولی افتاد مشکل‌ها
ما در پیاله عکس رخ یار دیده‌ایم
ای بی‌خبر ز لذت شرب مدام ما"
"Come, O Cupbearer, pass the cup / For love seemed easy at first, but difficulties fell. / We have seen the reflection of the Beloved's face in the cup, / O you unaware of the joy of our constant drinking." The whole Divan runs on this double reading: wine is wine and wine is ecstasy. The cupbearer is a server and a spiritual guide. Hafez never resolves which is which. The ambiguity is the device. His enemies couldn't censor him because the literal reading was always available as cover.
🎤 Extended Kinaya, Kendrick Lamar
"How much a dollar cost?
The question is detrimental, paralyzin' my thoughts
...
You're lookin' at the Messiah, the son of Jehovah, the higher power
The choir that spoke the word, the Holy Spirit
The nerve of Nazareth, and I'll tell you just how much a dollar cost
The price of having a spot in Heaven, embrace your loss—I am God"
"How Much a Dollar Cost" (2015). For three full verses, the song is literally about a homeless man asking Kendrick for a dollar outside a gas station, and Kendrick refusing. The literal reading is complete: an uncomfortable encounter, guilt, rationalizations. The implied reading surfaces only at the end when the homeless man says "I am God." Every prior detail recontextualizes: every refusal was a theological failure, every rationalization a spiritual one. The dollar was never about money. Hafez's wine held two meanings simultaneously across a whole Divan. Kendrick holds two meanings across a single song and detonates the hidden one at the final line.
