The Word That Comes Back Every Time
ردیف (Radif)

Persian poetry's most distinctive feature, a phrase repeated identically at the end of every single line. English has no name for this. English poetry avoids it. Music never stopped using it.

Let's talk about repetition, specifically the kind English poetry teachers would mark in red, and Persian poets would consider a mark of mastery.

The radif (ردیف) is a word or group of words that appears identically at the end of each line of a ghazal, after the rhyme word. Not near-identical. Not a variation. The exact same word or phrase, every time. In a ghazal of ten couplets, you might see the word "again", or "what became of you", or "in this city", appearing at the end of every single line. Twenty times. Exactly the same.

Your first instinct might be: that sounds monotonous. That instinct is English conditioning talking. In the Persian tradition, the radif does something completely different. Each time it lands, it means something slightly new because the line before it has changed. The radif is a fixed point. The entire poem orbits it. The repetition doesn't exhaust the word, it deepens it.

Why Repetition Deepens Instead of Boring

Here's the mechanism. When a word repeats in a fixed position, it starts to accumulate meaning. By the fourth or fifth appearance, the listener isn't just hearing the word, they're hearing every previous appearance simultaneously. The word becomes a vessel. The poem fills it over time.

Imagine a poem where every couplet ends with the word "again", and each couplet is about a different kind of return: the lover coming back, the season returning, memory returning, grief returning, the same mistake returning. By the final couplet, "again" doesn't mean any single one of those returns. It means all of them at once. The radif has been stretched to hold everything the poem is about.

🔁

Sufis would recognize this as zikr, the practice of repeating a divine name until it empties of ordinary meaning and fills with presence. Classical Persian poets did both at once: technical form and spiritual practice in the same structural move. The radif as meditation.

الا یا ایها الساقی ادر کأسا و ناولها
که عشق آسان نمود اول ولی افتاد مشکل‌ها
به بوی نافه‌ای کاخر صبا زان طرّه بگشاید
ز تاب جعد مشکینش چه خون افتاد در دل‌ها
Hafez's most famous ghazal. The radif is "-ha" (ها), the Persian plural suffix that becomes the recurring anchor. "Mashkil-ha" (difficulties), "del-ha" (hearts), each couplet introduces something new; the -ha returns like a tide. By the end of the ghazal, that small sound carries the weight of everything the poem has accumulated.

The Closest English Equivalent: The Hook

English poetry doesn't have the radif, but music does. The chorus of a pop song is a radif. The hook in hip-hop is a radif. The repeated line in a folk ballad is a radif. The device is universal in music; it just didn't make it into the English literary tradition, which valued variety at line endings over constancy.

🇦🇫 Radif in Music, Ahmad Zahir
"من که باشم که بپرسم ز تو حال دل خویش
من که باشم که بگویم غم و احوال دل خویش
تو که خود حال پریشان دلم را می‌دانی
چه بگویم ز پریشانی احوال دل خویش"
"Who am I to ask you the state of my own heart / Who am I to tell you the grief and condition of my own heart / You who already know the troubled state of my heart, / What should I say about the disorder of my own heart?", Ahmad Zahir. "Del-e khoysh" (my own heart) functions as a radif across every verse. The phrase gains weight with each return, loaded with more acceptance and resignation by the song's end than it carried at the beginning.
🎵 Musical Radif, Bob Dylan
"How many roads must a man walk down
before you call him a man?
The answer, my friend, is blowin' in the wind
The answer is blowin' in the wind"
"Blowin' in the Wind." The phrase closes every verse. By the final repetition, it has accumulated the unanswered questions of freedom, war, and human dignity. "Blowin' in the wind" is bigger by the last chorus than it was in the first, the poem has filled the vessel. Same mechanism as the Persian radif. Dylan just called it a chorus.
🇮🇷 Radif, Forugh Farrokhzad
"تولدی دیگر
من در زهدان جهان زاده شدم
و هر بار از نو زاده می‌شوم
تولدی دیگر، تولدی دیگر"
"Another Birth", Forugh Farrokhzad. The phrase "another birth / tawallodi digar" threads through her entire collection, functioning as a radif at the level of the whole book rather than individual lines. Each poem that uses it adds a new layer: birth as transformation, birth as defiance, birth as grief, birth as self-authorship. By the collection's end, the phrase holds all of it.
🎵 Musical Radif, Beyoncé
"I woke up like this, I woke up like this
We flawless, ladies tell 'em
I woke up like this, I woke up like this
We flawless, ladies tell 'em"
"***Flawless" (2013). "I woke up like this" accumulates meaning across the song, it starts as a statement about natural beauty, and by the end it means: defiance of the male gaze, Black womanhood, self-sufficiency, refusal to perform. The radif mechanism deepens it. Same line, each time larger.

The Monorhyme Alongside the Radif

Alongside the radif, classical Persian poetry in forms like the qasida sustains a single rhyme for sometimes a hundred couplets. English readers tend to hear this as constraint. Persian listeners hear it as commitment, the way a blues guitarist sounds completely different over the same 12-bar progression every time, or the way a jazz musician can take a standard and make it entirely their own.

"در ازل پرتو حُسنت ز تجلّی دم زد
عشق پیدا شد و آتش به همه عالم زد
عقل می‌خواست کز آن شعله چراغ افروزد
برق غیرت بزد و دودمان عالم زد"

Hafez, "In eternity, the radiance of Your beauty breathed forth in revelation / Love appeared, and set fire to the whole world / Reason wanted to light a lamp from that flame / But the lightning of jealousy struck and burned the world's household." The radif structure of this ghazal mirrors its theological content: the repeated return to the same ending word enacts the cyclical nature of divine emanation the poem is describing. Form and meaning are not separate.

🎸

Try this listening experiment: Next time a song's final chorus hits differently than the first one, same words, more weight, that's the radif mechanism. The song has filled the phrase with everything that came before it. Hafez's readers felt exactly this in the maqta (final couplet) of a ghazal. The device is older than any of us, and it still works every time.
