She's Like the Moon (14 Ways)
تشبیه (Tashbih)

Persian scholars spent a thousand years categorizing every possible type of comparison. English class stops at two. Here's what they found, and why it matters.

You know what a simile is. You compare one thing to another because they share a quality. "Her voice is like honey." "His laugh sounds like a broken drain." You've had this since primary school.

Now imagine spending a thousand years asking: what kind of comparison is it? Is the shared quality stated or hidden? Is the comparison equal, or does one side obviously win? What happens when you build a comparison and then immediately flip it on yourself? What if the thing you're comparing to turns out to be worse than the thing you're describing?

That obsession is the history of Tashbih (تشبیه). The Bahar-e Adab study we're drawing on traces it across a thousand years: early medieval texts recognized a handful of types; by the 14th century, scholars had named over a dozen, and the list kept expanding because poets kept doing new things that the existing vocabulary couldn't describe.

The Four Parts of Any Comparison

Persian scholars gave every piece of a simile a precise name. Once you know these four, you can see exactly how any comparison is built, and understand why some hit harder than others.

Mushabba, مشبّه

The subject

"Her face…", whatever you're comparing FROM. The thing being described.

Mushabba Bihi, مشبّه به

The standard

"…like the moon." What you're comparing it TO. The reference point.

Adat, ادات

The particle

"Like," "as," "resembles." In Persian: مانند، چون، همچو. The word that signals a comparison is happening.

Wajh-e Shabe, وجه شبه

The ground

The shared quality, brightness, softness, distance. Often left unstated. Its absence is where the power lives.

💡

In 1936, the English critic I.A. Richards named the same three parts: tenor, vehicle, and ground. Persian scholars had their version a thousand years earlier. Same insight, different clock.

The Types, Each One Does Something Different

Tashbih-e Mujmal, Leave the Reason Out

The most powerful similes often leave the shared quality unstated. "Her cheeks are like roses", you don't explain the redness or the softness or the perishability. You let the reader feel their way there. The act of finding the connection is what makes it stick.

🇮🇷 Open-ended, Rumi
"بشنو این نی چون شکایت می‌کند
از جدایی‌ها حکایت می‌کند
کز نیستان تا مرا ببریده‌اند
از نفیرم مرد و زن نالیده‌اند"
"Listen to this reed, how it complains / From separations it tells its tales / Since they cut me from the reed bed / Every man and woman has mourned alongside me." The reed flute is the soul. Rumi doesn't explain why, he drops the ground and lets you feel your way to it. The silence around the comparison is the poem.
🎵 Open-ended, Leonard Cohen
"Ring the bells that still can ring
Forget your perfect offering
There is a crack in everything
That's how the light gets in"
Cohen, "Anthem." The crack as a simile for human imperfection, but he doesn't say that. He gives you the image and trusts you to make the connection. The "bells that still can ring" implies damaged ones, himself, all of us. Ground unstated, resonance total.
📖 Open-ended, Sa'di
"گل همین پنج روز و شش باشد
وین گلستان همیشه خوش باشد
مرو ای دوست کز جهان رفتی
که جهان را بقاست و تو نه‌ای"
"The flower lasts only five or six days / But this rose garden is always beautiful / Don't go, friend, since you have left the world, / The world endures and you do not." The flower as life, but Sa'di places the two images side by side and lets you draw the line. Everything fragile, everything that endures. The comparison is in the gap between the lines.
📖 Open-ended, Emily Dickinson
"'Hope' is the thing with feathers—
That perches in the soul—
And sings the tune without the words—
And never stops—at all—"
Dickinson, c. 1861. Hope compared to a bird, but the ground is never stated. Why a feathered thing? Why perching? The comparison arrives before any explanation, and the explanation never comes. What hope does — enduring without being asked, singing without an audience — is the whole point, and Dickinson trusts the image to carry it. Same instinct as Sa'di: drop the ground, let the reader feel their way to it.
🇮🇷 Open-ended, Hafez (the candle)
"چون شمع می‌گریم در این محفل که دیده‌ام
از آتش اشکم چو شمع پیش از تو آب شوم
شمع می‌گرید و می‌سوزد و می‌ریزد اشک
گوی این گریه و این سوز از بهر کیست"
"I weep in this gathering like a candle / From the fire of my tears I will melt before you like a candle / The candle weeps and burns and sheds tears / As if to ask: for whose sake is this weeping, this burning?" Hafez. The ground of the candle simile is never stated. You feel it before you name it: a candle burns itself to give others light, it melts in public, it weeps wax, it's consumed by the very fire that makes it visible. None of that is said. The image lands and the reader supplies everything. This is tashbih-e mujmal at its most economical — a three-hundred-year tradition of Persian poets reaching for the same object because its unstated grounds are so obvious they don't need stating.
🎵 Open-ended, Taylor Swift
"So casually cruel in the name of bein' honest
I'm a crumpled-up piece of paper lyin' here
'Cause I remember it all, all, all"
"All Too Well (10 Minute Version)" (2012). Subject: Taylor, post-relationship, post-confrontation. Standard: a crumpled piece of paper. Comparison particle: "I'm." Ground: completely unstated. She doesn't explain why she's like crumpled paper. She doesn't need to. The ground activates on contact: something that was written on and then discarded. Something that held words once and now holds only the shape of having been crushed. Something that could be smoothed out but never look the same again. All of that in four words. Sa'di's flower lasts five days before it's gone. The paper was thrown away and it's still lying there. Two images of damage across six centuries — neither one explains itself.

Tashbih-e Ma'kus, The Reversal

The poet makes a comparison and then flips it. "I compared your face to the moon, but then I realized: the moon is only beautiful because it reminds me of your face." The compliment escalates by demoting the very standard used to make it.

🎭 Reversal, Shakespeare, Sonnet 18
"Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?
Thou art more lovely and more temperate.
Rough winds do shake the darling buds of May,
And summer's lease hath all too short a date."
Shakespeare opens by offering the comparison, then immediately downgrades it. The summer's day has flaws, rough winds, a short lease. The beloved has none of these. This is tashbih-e ma'kus in English: the comparison is proposed, then the standard is found lacking in the same breath.
🇮🇷 Reversal, Hafez
"هر که دید آن رخ و زلف از پی آن تافته
گفت کو آفتاب و کو شب تافته
آفتاب و ماه را خجل کند
رخ چون گل و زلف چون شب تافته"
"Whoever saw that face and those curls went searching, where is the sun now? Where is the moonlit night? / That rose-like face and night-like curls / Make both the sun and moon ashamed." After seeing the beloved, the sun and moon become the things being measured. The comparison reverses direction entirely.

Tashbih-e Sharti, The Conditional

The comparison only works in a hypothetical world. By saying what would need to change about the standard to match the subject, the poet implies: nothing in reality quite measures up.

بسرو ماند اگر بود دار لاله سرو
بمورد ماند اگر مورد روید از نسرین
"It would resemble a cypress if the cypress bore tulip flowers / It would resemble a myrtle if the myrtle grew from jasmine", Rudaki, father of Persian poetry (9th century). The conditional shrinks every standard. The beloved exceeds every category. This is the oldest trick in the book, making a compliment by pointing out what doesn't exist.

Tashbih-e Muzawwaj, The Chain

Multiple comparisons linked into one structure, not just A like B, but "A is to B as C is to D." The comparisons build off each other, creating a web rather than a single point.

📖 Chain, Allama Iqbal (Urdu)
"ستاروں سے آگے جہاں اور بھی ہیں
ابھی عشق کے امتحاں اور بھی ہیں
تہی زندگی سے نہیں یہ فضائیں
یہاں سینکڑوں کارواں اور بھی ہیں"
"Beyond the stars there are other worlds / There are still more trials of love ahead / These skies are not empty of life / Here too are hundreds more caravans." Iqbal links space to love to endurance, each comparison amplifies the others. The universe is a simile for longing, and longing is a simile for infinite possibility. They loop, building a chain.
🎵 Chain, Bob Dylan
"How many roads must a man walk down
before you call him a man?
Yes, and how many seas must a white dove sail
before she sleeps in the sand?"
"Blowin' in the Wind." The chain of parallel "how many" questions builds a compound simile for injustice, each question is a different version of the same impossible waiting. The structure is the argument. Dylan's chain and Iqbal's chain are structurally the same move.

More Similes, Across the Whole Map

🇮🇷 Epic Simile, Ferdowsi, Shahnameh
"چو شیر ژیان در میان سپاه
همی رفت پیروز با فرّ و جاه
چو باد بهار از بر کوهسار
بیامد بر آن رزمگه بر نثار"
"Like a roaring lion in the midst of the army / He advanced, victorious, with glory and power / Like the spring wind from the mountaintop / He came scattering over that battlefield." Ferdowsi stacks two similes, lion for power, spring wind for speed, because one wasn't enough. Homer does the exact same thing for Achilles in the Iliad. Independent discovery, ten centuries apart.
🏛️ Kinship Simile, Homer
"As is the generation of leaves, so too of men:
the wind scatters the leaves upon the ground,
but the growing forest burgeons with others
as the season of spring comes on."
Homer, Iliad, Book VI (c. 8th century BCE). Glaucus delivers this to a warrior about to kill him — men like leaves, the shared quality (brevity, beauty, replacement by others) is never stated. The comparison does more than any argument could: life is brief, and part of a cycle bigger than either of us. A kinship simile where the two things are linked not by a word but by a situation. Homer didn't know Ferdowsi would reach for the same structure a thousand years later. He just knew the move.
🇵🇰 Ghalib, Fire simile (Urdu)
"عشق پر زور نہیں ہے یہ وہ آتش غالب
کہ لگائے نہ لگے اور بجھائے نہ بجھے
آگ ہے سب سے پہلے دل کے اندر
پھر جلتا ہے جہاں بھی آشیانہ ہے"
"Love cannot be forced, it is a fire, Ghalib / That won't catch when you try, and won't die when you want / First the fire is inside the heart / Then it burns wherever there is a nest." The fire's behavior is the comparison, not just its heat or light, but its refusal to obey. A structural simile: the situation of fire parallels the situation of love.
📖 Structural Simile, Emily Dickinson
"Because I could not stop for Death—
He kindly stopped for me—
The Carriage held but just Ourselves—
And Immortality."
Dickinson, c. 1862. Death compared to a gentleman caller who takes her for a carriage ride. Not just an image, a situation: his behavior (courteous, unhurried, patient) is the comparison. The carriage slows past the school, the fields, the setting sun. Death doesn't stalk. He waits. The fire's behavior was Ghalib's whole comparison. The suitor's behavior is Dickinson's. A structural simile needs the situation to match, not just the object, and Dickinson's situation matches one of the oldest truths in Persian poetry: that the thing you fear most is also, somehow, the most courteous guest.

Tashbih-e Tamsil, The Analogy-Simile

The strongest similes don't compare object to object, they compare situation to situation. The ground isn't a single shared quality (bright, soft, fast) but a whole logical relationship. Persian calls this tashbih-e tamsil, the analogy-simile. Lupe Fiasco lives in this mode: his comparisons are little machines, not pictures.

🏔️ Tashbih, Rudaki (c. 940 CE)
"بوی جوی مولیان آید همی
یاد یار مهربان آید همی
ریگ آموی و درشتی راه او
زیر پایم پرنیان آید همی"
"The scent of the Moliyan stream keeps coming / The memory of a kind friend keeps coming / The sand of the Amu and the roughness of its road / Feels like silk beneath my feet." The simile is in the last line: rough sand compared to parniyan (fine silk). But the ground isn't a shared physical quality, sand isn't soft. It's longing for home that transforms hardship into softness. The situation carries the comparison, not the object. Exactly Lupe's move, a thousand years earlier.

"Mural" (2015). None of these are picture-similes; they're analogy-similes. Roosters don't fly like boosters don't buy: just as a rooster can't fly, a "booster" (shoplifter) never pays, situation mapped onto situation, with the internal rhyme roosters/boosters locking it shut. The Same Instinct, Across Every Tradition

Persian tashbih didn't appear in isolation. Across the Silk Road, in the Tang courts of China, in the haiku tradition of Japan, in ancient Greece and classical India, poets were working out the same problems under different names. The categories are different. The device is identical.

🏮 Open Simile, Li Bai (701–762 CE)
"Before my bed, bright moonlight—
I take it for frost upon the ground.
Raising my head, I gaze at the bright moon;
Bowing my head, I think of home."
"Quiet Night Thought" (静夜思), Li Bai, Tang Dynasty. The simile is in the first two lines: moonlight compared to frost. The shared quality (cold, pale, spreading silence) is unstated. Then the poem pivots away from the comparison into longing. The simile was just the door. Li Bai opens it and walks through into something else. Tashbih-e mujmal at its most economical: twenty Chinese characters, one image, a thousand years of readers thinking of home.
🎑 Compressed Simile, Matsuo Basho (1644–1694)
"An old silent pond—
A frog jumps into the pond.
Splash! Silence again."
Basho, 1686. The haiku contains no explicit comparison, but the structure IS one: the ancient pond (permanence, stillness) placed beside the frog's moment (disruption, sound), with silence immediately returning. Two things set next to each other without the comparison word, the reader feels the tension in the gap. This is tashbih compressed so far the "like" has evaporated entirely. Persian poets called this type the strongest kind. Basho arrived at the same conclusion from the opposite direction.
🏛️ Reversal Simile, Sappho (c. 620–570 BCE)
"Some say an army of horsemen,
some say infantry, some say a fleet
is the finest thing on the dark earth—
I say it is whatever one loves."
Sappho, Fragment 16. The chain of "some say" examples (armies, infantry, fleet) are proposed standards, each one then demoted. Whatever one loves is the final term that defeats all of them. This is tashbih-e ma'kus, the reversal: the supposed standards are raised and then found lacking, because the actual subject exceeds every category. Hafez made the sun and moon ashamed. Sappho made armies irrelevant. Two poets, two islands, twenty-five hundred years apart, working the same reversal.
🪷 Chain Simile, Kalidasa (4th–5th century CE)
"As the dark cloud brings relief to the parched earth,
as the rain revives what the sun has dried—
so the word from a beloved place
reaches one in exile like rain after drought."
Kalidasa, Meghaduta (Cloud Messenger), Sanskrit. The chain simile links cloud to rain to the beloved's message: each term amplifies the same feeling (relief after deprivation) until the emotion accumulates beyond what any single image could hold. Iqbal and Kalidasa were both mapping longing onto the cosmos, centuries apart and several languages east of each other. The Meghaduta's entire premise is a simile: use the monsoon cloud as a messenger because longing and rain both travel across impossible distances toward what they love.
