فصاحت · Fasahat

The Reason Some Lines Just Feel Right
فصاحت (Fasahat)

Fasahat, eloquence, is the foundation. Before any of the clever devices, there's this: language that sounds right, fits the moment, and carries no dead weight. Both traditions agree on the rules, even when they disagree on everything else.

We've covered the flashy stuff, simile, metaphor, double meaning, wordplay, the radif. All of it only works if the basic language underneath is right. That basic rightness has a name in Persian: fasahat (فصاحت).

Fasahat means eloquence, but not showy eloquence. It means language that is clear, fitting, appropriate, and pleasant to say and hear. Taqavi's Hanjaar-e Goftar opens with fasahat because without it, all the Bayan and Badi' in the world is just expensive noise. The foundation has to be solid before the architecture can be beautiful.

And here's the thing: the rules both traditions lay down for fasahat are almost identical. They arrived at them independently. Because the problems fasahat solves are universal, they're not Persian problems or English problems, they're human problems with language.

Rule One: If It's Hard to Say, Rewrite It

Both Persian and English rhetoric flag the same problem: putting sounds next to each other that produce a harsh, difficult result. In Persian this is called tanafur-e huruf (تنافر حروف), the friction of letters. Dental consonants clustered together, aspirates colliding, vowels stopping and starting awkwardly. The result is a line that stumbles in the mouth.

English composition handbooks say the same thing. "The student's strengths stretched across several sessions", technically correct, practically a tongue-twister. Both traditions have the same practical test: read it out loud. If your mouth struggles, your reader's ear will too.

🎵 Good Sound, Rumi
"بشنو این نی چون شکایت می‌کند
از جدایی‌ها حکایت می‌کند
کز نیستان تا مرا ببریده‌اند
از نفیرم مرد و زن نالیده‌اند"
"Listen to the reed, how it complains / From separations it tells its tales / Since they cut me from the reed bed / Every man and woman has mourned alongside me." Read this aloud in Persian: the nasal "n" sounds, the soft "sh," the flowing "m", the sound enacts what the words describe. The reed's voice IS the music of the line. Fasahat: when sound and meaning fuse.
🎵 Good Sound, Keats
"Season of mists and mellow fruitfulness,
Close bosom-friend of the maturing sun;
Conspiring with him how to load and bless
With fruit the vines that round the thatch-eves run"
Keats, "To Autumn." The "m" and "f" alliteration, the long vowels, the softness of "mellow" and "fruitfulness", it's physically pleasant to say. Keats reportedly composed while walking and reciting, not sitting and writing. He knew the mouth was the judge, not the eye.

Rule Two: Use Words That Belong in the Room

In Persian, this is gharabat-e lughat (غرابت لغات), the strangeness of vocabulary. Don't use foreign words that haven't been naturalized, archaic words no one knows, or technical terms dropped without context into emotional writing. The word might be accurate; it still doesn't belong.

In English: register mismatch. Using corporate jargon in a love poem. Medical terminology in a lullaby. Slang in a legal argument. The word is correct; the room is wrong.

🇮🇷 Right word, right room, Hafez
"یک قدح می باید و دیوانگی
کشتن آن پیر عقل خود بینی
پیر ما گفت خطا بر قلم صنع نرفت
آفرین بر نظر پاک خطاپوشش باد"
"One cup of wine is needed, and madness / To kill that old, self-seeing reason. / Our elder said: no error passed through the pen of creation, / Bless his pure gaze that covers up faults." Every word is common, clear, and carries weight. "Old" and "self-seeing" do double work in three syllables, describing reason as decrepit AND narcissistic. No foreign insertions, no jargon. The vocabulary fits the room exactly.
📖 Right word, right room, Walt Whitman
"Do I contradict myself?
Very well then I contradict myself,
(I am large, I contain multitudes.)"
Whitman, "Song of Myself" (1855). Democratic American vernacular mixed with philosophical scope: "large" is ordinary, "multitudes" is grand, and the combination works because Whitman built the whole poem in that register, plain speech doing the work of epic. The parenthesis at the end, repeating the line after the argument, is maqam: this is a poem about self and contradiction, so the form contradicts itself. Every word is in the right room, and the room was designed so that the contradiction fits.

Rule Three: Cut the Filler

Hashw (حشو) is filler, words added to a line to complete the meter or fill the syllable count that don't add meaning. One of the classic sins of Persian verse, catalogued by Taqavi as a mark of weakness. "A verse containing hashw is like a body with unnecessary limbs: technically complete, but actually deformed."

English composition: padding. "In order to," "it is important to note that," "for all intents and purposes." Writing that reaches the word count without earning it. Every writing teacher you've ever had said this. Persian critics were saying it in the 10th century.

✂️ Zero filler, Omar Khayyam
"یک قدح می باید و دیوانگی
کشتن آن پیر عقل خود بینی
می‌خور که هر کی نخورد باده پشیمان شود
در آخر که نه کس ماند نه کاشانه"
"One cup of wine is needed and madness / To kill that old self-seeing reason / Drink, for whoever doesn't drink wine will regret it / In the end, neither anyone remains nor any home." Khayyam. Every word is load-bearing. "Old" and "self-seeing" describe reason as both decrepit and narcissistic in the same breath. The final line, "neither anyone nor any home", compresses the whole human condition into six words.
✂️ Zero filler, Henry David Thoreau
"I went to the woods because I wished to live deliberately,
to front only the essential facts of life,
and see if I could not learn what it had to teach,
and not, when I came to die, discover that I had not lived."
Thoreau, Walden (1854). Every word earns its place. "Deliberately" does the work of three sentences. "Front" (to face directly, to meet head-on) is one syllable carrying confrontation and intention. "Discover that I had not lived" at the end compresses a whole philosophy of regret into seven words. No hashw. The rhythm holds because nothing could be removed without loss. Khayyam and Thoreau were both asking the same question from opposite sides of the world: if you could live deliberately, would you? Both answered in prose that couldn't afford to waste a word.

Rule Four: Match the Occasion

The concept of maqam (مقام), situational appropriateness, is both a Ma'ani principle and a fasahat requirement. A technically perfect line in the wrong context is still a failure of eloquence. Classical Persian court poetry had elaborate conventions about which poems fit which occasions: qasidas for royal praise, ghazals for intimate gatherings, marsiyya for mourning. Being out of register was a craft failure, not just a social one.

🇵🇰 Maqam, Iqbal at the right moment
"خودی کو کر بلند اتنا کہ ہر تقدیر سے پہلے
خدا بندے سے خود پوچھے، بتا تیری رضا کیا ہے
ستاروں سے آگے جہاں اور بھی ہیں
ابھی عشق کے امتحاں اور بھی ہیں"
"Raise yourself so high that before each decree of fate / God Himself asks you: tell me, what is your will? / Beyond the stars there are other worlds / There are still more trials of love ahead.", Iqbal writing for a Muslim community at a particular political and historical moment. These lines were recited at independence movements, memorized by children, sung at public gatherings. The maqam, the occasion, shaped the directness, the urgency, the grandiosity. The same content written for a private notebook would need a different register.
🎭 Maqam, Shakespeare, different rooms
"Friends, Romans, countrymen, lend me your ears;
I come to bury Caesar, not to praise him.
The evil that men do lives after them;
The good is oft interred with their bones."
Mark Antony's funeral oration, Julius Caesar. This is maqam in English: a public speech in a public moment, and Shakespeare gives it the public register it requires, direct address, short sentences, rhetorical rhythm, irony that the crowd can follow. Compare it to Hamlet's soliloquy: same playwright, completely different occasion, completely different register. That flexibility IS the craft.

"The goal of all these rules, sound, vocabulary, filler, occasion, is ultimately to make the language disappear. The most eloquent writing is the kind where you forget you're reading. You feel you're experiencing directly."

The paradox at the heart of both traditions: all these rules exist so that, eventually, you don't notice any of them. When a verse is perfectly constructed, the construction disappears. You just feel the thing the verse is about. That's fasahat at its summit: language that earns its own absence.

Breaking One Rule to Keep the Deeper One

Fasahat has an opposite, and a name for it: ta'qid (تعقید), willful obscurity, the line so tangled the reader can't get through it. Classical critics list it as a failure. But there's a paradox the tradition itself half-admits: Sab'k-e Hindi made controlled difficulty a virtue. Bedil is hard on purpose. The trick is that the difficulty must never come from hashw (filler), it has to come from compression. Break the clarity rule, but obey the no-waste rule absolutely. Lupe Fiasco's "Mural" is the clearest modern case of exactly this bargain.

🇦🇫 Productive Ta'qid, Bedil (Dari)
"نه ما دانیم و نه ایشان که ما را کی رها کردند
در این وحشت‌سرا دل‌های ما دیوانه‌وار آمد
نه از خود بود و نه از غیر بود این آمدن ما
همان مهمان‌نوازی بود که دعوت داشت"
"Neither we know, nor they, when they released us / In this wilderness our hearts arrived like madmen / Our coming was neither from ourselves nor another, / It was only a hospitality that had issued an invitation." Bedil is deliberately hard, the "they," the "release," the "invitation" are all left unresolved. But run the test: try to cut a single word. You can't. The difficulty is pure compression, not padding. That's ta'qid as craft, not as failure.

"Mural" (2015). An eight-minute verse with no hook and no chorus, the most obscure thing in his catalogue, and yet not one syllable is filler. Lupe even states the fasahat ethic out loud mid-song: guard the voice like a fortress, treat each word like a military reinforcement, something deployed for a reason, never dead weight. He breaks the surface rule of fasahat (be clear) while obeying its deepest rule (waste nothing). Exactly Bedil's bargain: the difficulty is earned because the economy underneath it is total.

Fasahat Without Borders

Persian critics formalized these rules in the 9th century. But the underlying problem, how do you make language disappear into experience, is one every tradition solved independently. The names change. The conclusions don't.

🏛️ Sound and Sense, Horace (65–8 BCE)
"Dum loquimur, fugerit invida
aetas: carpe diem, quam minimum
credula postero."
"While we speak, envious time will have fled: seize the day, trust as little as possible in tomorrow." Horace, Odes I.11. The Latin sound is doing fasahat work: "fugerit" (will have fled) comes in the middle of the sentence, at the moment the thing it describes happens — the line enacts its meaning. "Carpe diem" is two syllables. It contains everything the next paragraph would say. No hashw. Horace composed his odes as slowly and deliberately as any Persian ghazal. Both traditions agreed: the poem should sound like it couldn't be shorter.
🪷 Zero Filler, Thiruvalluvar (c. 1st century BCE–CE)
"The greatness of a man who has conquered himself
exceeds the greatness of a man who has conquered the world."
Thiruvalluvar, Tirukkural, Book I (Tamil). Each of the 1,330 Kural couplets is exactly two lines. No exceptions. The formal constraint forces every word to earn its place — the Tamil tradition made anti-hashw a structural requirement rather than an aspiration. This couplet contains a tibaq (self vs. world), a complete argument, and a reversal of expected value (inner over outer). Two lines. Nothing extra. Taqavi called this kind of economy "the most advanced fasahat": when the constraint and the content become the same thing.
