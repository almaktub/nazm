A Cross-Cultural Deep Dive
هنر کلام · Honar-e Kalam

What do Hafez, Shakespeare,
and Kendrick Lamar
have in common?

They all used the same handful of tricks. They just called them different things, and lived about 600 years apart. This site unpacks those tricks.

"بشنو این نی چون شکایت می‌کند
از جدایی‌ها حکایت می‌کند
کز نیستان تا مرا ببریده‌اند
از نفیرم مرد و زن نالیده‌اند"

Rumi, Masnavi, "Listen to the reed flute, how it tells a tale of separations / Since they cut me from the reed bed, every man and woman has mourned alongside me." The same ache that runs through every great song, in any language.

There's a version of literary history where Persian and English poetry never meet, Hafez over here with his ghazals and wine, Shakespeare over there with his sonnets and tragedies, two ships passing in the night. That version is lazy and wrong.

The tools of great writing, comparison, metaphor, double meaning, sound work, repetition, show up in every tradition because they come from the same place: the human need to say something that straight description just can't handle. When words aren't enough, you reach for a figure of speech. That's true in 13th-century Persia. It's true in 21st-century Compton.

🇦🇫 Ahmad Zahir, Afghanistan, 1970s
"دلم گرفته از این شهر پر ملال و غبار
کجاست آن رفیق همدرد و آن دیار
کجاست آن یار قدیمی که دل من در بند اوست
جایگاه او کجا شد، رفت و آمد کرد چه کرد"
"My heart is heavy in this sad, dusty city / Where is that understanding friend, that homeland / Where is that old companion my heart is bound to / What became of his place, he came and went, what did he do?", Afghanistan's most beloved singer, using imagery straight out of 12th-century court poetry, in a pop song. Nothing has changed.
🎵 Bob Dylan, USA, 1963
"How many roads must a man walk down
before you call him a man?
Yes, and how many seas must a white dove sail
before she sleeps in the sand?"
"Blowin' in the Wind." Questions that aren't really questions, they carry the weight of everything that can't be said directly. Same move as Zahir. Different dust, same longing. The Persian term for this is kinaya. Dylan didn't need to know the word.
🇵🇰 Mirza Ghalib, Delhi, 1850s
"ہزاروں خواہشیں ایسی کہ ہر خواہش پے دم نکلے
بہت نکلے میرے ارمان لیکن پھر بھی کم نکلے
ڈرے کیوں میرا قاتل کیا رہے گا اس کا کیا
وہ خون جو چشم تر میں بعد مرگ بھی نہ نکلے"
"Thousands of desires, each worth dying for / Many were fulfilled, yet I still want more / Why should my killer fear, what will happen to him? / That blood which even tears after death could not bring out.", The original "it's never enough," written 170 years before anyone put it on an album.
🎤 The Rolling Stones, UK, 1965
"I can't get no satisfaction
though I try and I try and I try and I try
I can't get no satisfaction
and I ain't gonna face no defeat"
Mick Jagger's version of Ghalib's problem. The antithesis (satisfaction vs. endless trying), the repetition as emphasis, same rhetorical tools, different century, different continent. The Persian term for the paired opposites is tibaq. Jagger was doing it anyway.
